Limit Order vs. Stop Order

By Triston Martin Updated on 2022-08-29 00:00:00
Several distinct order types allow you to communicate your trading preferences to your broker. You can notify your broker that you are not interested in buying or selling at the current market price by giving them instructions to execute your order only at the specified limit or stop price. A limit order is different from a stop order in two crucial respects.

You may specify to your broker how you want your transactions executed by using a variety of order types. By instructing your broker to execute your order only at the specified limit or stop price, you are telling them that you are not interested in buying or selling at the current market price—two key distinctions separate limit orders from stop orders.

The first is that a limit order's price specifies the lowest possible price at which the transaction will take place, whereas a stop order's price only triggers an actual order after the price has been transacted.

Limit Orders

In stock trading, a limit order is an order to purchase or sell at a predetermined price. Set a limit order if you wish to buy $100 worth of stock at or below $100, and your order won't be honored until that price, or lower becomes available.

However, you can't just place a limit order to purchase shares at a price higher than the current market price since a better price is already out there. You may do the same thing with stock sales by placing a limit order to sell at a specific price.

Stop Orders

Although stop orders can be set in a few different ways, they all have conditions that rely on a price that is not yet accessible in the market. A stop order will be activated once the future price is known, and the broker will act accordingly.

Traders can clarify that their stop order will only be executed at an actual quoted price in the market by placing it with a broker with "stop on quote" as one of its order types. If you place a stop order with a $100 stop price, it will be executed if a valid quotation is received at or above $100.

Stop-Limit Orders

To place a stop-limit order, you must provide both a stop price and a limit price. When the specified stop price is reached, the order will trigger a limit order to purchase or sell the security. 2 To illustrate, let's say you invest $100 in stock with the expectation that it would increase in value.

You may set a stop-limit order to sell the shares if your prognosis is inaccurate. The order will be executed if the stock price falls to $90 or below the maximum price of $90.50. In contrast, if the market meets your requested limit price, your limit order will be executed.

There are no buyers at your limit price of $90.50 per share. Therefore even if the stock decreases overnight to $89 per share, your order will be activated and wait to be filled. In this order type, the stop and limit prices might be the same.

The Purposes of Stop-Limit Orders in Trading

When a trader is not paying close attention to the market, a stop-limit order can be used to automatically place a buy or sell order when a specific price is reached. The order will be executed immediately after the target price is reached. Traders typically place one of two types of stop-limit orders:

Buy Stop Limit

A buy-stop limit is used to acquire a stock if the price hits a certain point. In this way, after a maximum price per share has been established, dealers can maintain tighter control over stock purchases. Once a trader decides the most they are ready to pay for a share of stock, they may set a stop price and a limit price. The limit price is the maximum price an investor is ready to pay per share, while the stop price is a price set lower than the current market price.

Sell Stop Limit

If the stock price drops to your stop price, you can conditionally tell your broker to sell the stock to you. There are two parts to a sell stop price: the stop price and the limit price. While the limit price is the lowest price a trader is ready to accept, the stop price is the price at which the limit order to sell is triggered.

Disadvantages of a Stop-Limit Order

While a stop-limit order can restrict losses and ensure a transaction at a set price, there are some dangers inherent with such an order. Among the dangers are:

No Execution

The transaction may not be performed even if a stop-limit order is placed, as the price may never rise over the limit price. Short-term attainment of a limit order may not guarantee its immediate execution if other orders are pending that would prefer to use all available stocks at the current price.

Partial Fills

For stock orders, a partial fill occurs when fewer than all shares in the order is traded. Splitting up an order for execution on many trading days can incur additional commissions, cutting down a trader's profits.
