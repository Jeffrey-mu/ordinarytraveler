Why Do Companies Care About Their Stock Prices?

By Triston Martin Updated on 2022-08-20 00:00:00
The price of a company's stock measures how optimistic buyers are about their prospects for increased profits. If the company's performance is reflected in the share price and shareholders are happy, the management team is more likely to remain in position and receive raises. For defensive reasons like fending off a takeover, a company may focus on the value of its stock. If a company's stock price grows following its overall success, it is more likely to receive positive publicity from financial experts and news outlets.

Companies trading on stock exchanges give stock prices a lot of thought because it is a proxy for the firm's financial health. A stock's future looks better the higher its price is.

Financial health analysts study changes in stock prices to assess businesses. To ascertain whether or not the stock price accurately reflects the company's performance, investors frequently examine the company's past earnings and the P/E ratio. This data will help investors and analysts evaluate the company's potential moving forward.

In an initial public offering (IPO), a company sells shares to the public through the primary market. Next, retail and institutional investors trade the equities on the secondary market (stock exchange).

The secondary market price of a company's stock does not affect the company's bottom line, either positively or negatively. In light of this, how much should a business care about the price of its stock? After all, it has already cashed in during the IPO's main market sale.

Financing

A company's finances will most likely expand significantly after its initial public offering (IPO). However, in the future, a company may require additional money to eliminate debt, expand current operations, or make strategic acquisitions. One way to do this is through equity financing, selling new shares to the general public. To attract new investors, the stock price must be high.

A company must be cautious about issuing too many new shares into circulation if it doesn't want to drive down the stock price. There may not be enough buyers in the market to absorb the supply of claims if there are too many.

Creditors also tend to look favorably upon companies with above-average share values since this is usually indicative of a successful organization. It's more likely that a company in this position will be approved for a loan at a preferable interest rate because it has a better chance of repaying its long-term debt.

Efficiency Evaluation of Upper Management

Analysts frequently check its stock price to determine a publicly listed company's health, financial stability, and market position. As the stock price rises, investors gain confidence that the company's management is successfully increasing profits.

Executives at the top of a firm are more inclined to stay put if they observe positive momentum in the company's stock price and investor sentiment. These high-ranking workers can also anticipate receiving annual raises in pay and bonus payments.

However, when a company's stock price drops, the board of directors may decide to replace some of the company's top executives. Management of any company would be wise to avoid celebrating a decline in share price.

Compensation

Similarly, the performance of the stock price of a firm as a whole is a significant factor in the incentive structure of the company's leadership. This is because stock options are a substantial source of income for many people in high management positions. These perks allow high-ranking executives to purchase business stock at a predetermined price and on a future date.

The option's value, however, will increase only if the underlying stock price increases. Therefore, it is essential for a company's success that stock options be made available to employees. To put it another way, CEOs are incentivized to make strategic decisions that raise the company's bottom line and, by extension, the value of stockholders' portfolios.

Possibility of Obtaining

One further incentive for a business to watch its stock price is to protect itself from a takeover. A falling stock price increases a company's desirability as a takeover target because of its lower market value.

Many different investors will typically hold stock in a publicly traded corporation. Therefore, when a target company's stock is trading at a lower price, potential acquirers can more easily afford to do so.

Keeping the stock price high is one-way management plans to discourage such actions. On the other hand, a company whose stock is trading at a premium has more room to maneuver in the market and may potentially acquire a competitor.

Reports and recommendations from financial experts and the media tend to be more positive when the company's share price is high. A company's market cap directly correlates to the amount of coverage it receives in the news. This cumulative effect lures additional investors and gives the business a much-needed cash injection.
