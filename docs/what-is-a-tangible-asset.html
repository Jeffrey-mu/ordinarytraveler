What Is a Tangible Asset?

By Triston Martin Updated on 2022-08-16 00:00:00
In contrast to intangible assets, tangible assets may be directly observed and evaluated by outside parties. Real estate, factories, and computers are all examples of physical assets. Businesses rely on their tangible assets, which allow them to create their products and services. For the simple reason that they are made of matter, natural disasters may cause damage to tangible things.

A tangible asset can be seen and touched and has a measurable monetary worth. While the liquidity of various markets will vary, tangible assets can generally always be transferred for some monetary value. In contrast to intangible assets, which have no practical worth outside of some theoretical calculation, physical assets may be touched, handled, and traded.

The assets of a company are crucial to its overall health and prosperity. Organizations typically keep a balance sheet to manage their assets better and the ramifications they may have on the business. The basic equation of assets minus liabilities equaling shareholders' equity dictates the balance sheet and requires a zero balance.

Tangible assets and intangible assets are both held by businesses. On the balance sheet, tangible assets are the bare minimum. In several sectors, they are the primary type of capital. In most cases, they are also the most intuitive and straightforward.

Tangible Assets, Both Present and Future

Both short- and long-term tangible assets exist. Existence on-site is not always a requirement for current assets, but the value of any given transaction must be limited. The cash on hand, cash equivalents, marketable securities, and accounts receivable of a firm are its most liquid, current tangible assets.

The quick ratio takes a company's total tangible assets into account. The current ratio of a corporation considers both cash and other short-term assets. The current ratio measures a company's ability to meet its short-term obligations using its available liquid assets.

Although inventory is not as easily converted into cash as are cash equivalents, it does have a finite market value and might be sold to raise cash if necessary; therefore, it is included in the current ratio of assets. The second component of the asset section of the balance sheet is made up of long-term assets.

Difference Between Tangible and Intangible Assets

Management of shareholders' equity and the related return on equity ratio relies heavily on asset value. All of a company's tangible and intangible assets make up its total asset base. So, the two numbers are added and examined as part of TPM. Non-tangible assets, like goodwill or patents, are considered intangible assets.

Copyrights, trademarks, patents, licenses, and brand value are all examples of intangible assets. The balance sheet shows intangible assets under the long-term assets section. The registration and renewal fees connected with intangible assets are two examples of specific values that may be used to determine their book value.

Tangible Asset Types

Companies with many physical assets may be found in many different sectors.

Manufacturing

Manufacturing companies like those in the car and steel sectors have physical assets. Tangible assets include the factory's machinery, computers, and physical structures.

Technology

Manufacturers of electrical products like cellphones, laptops, and tablets rely on physical facilities to create their wares.

Oil &' Gas Industry

Many oil and gas firms also have a sizable quantity of fixed assets. Oil exploration companies have their own oil rigs and drilling equipment. Companies in the oil industry are examples of those that are capital intensive, as they need large sums of money to buy equipment and other physical assets.

Types of Intangible Asset Companies

Companies in a variety of sectors have a disproportionate amount of intangible assets. Some of these are:

Technology

For computer and technology organizations, intangible assets such as copyrights, patents, key staff, and R&'D are crucial. As expected, Apple Inc. (AAPL) has intangible assets.

Entertainment

In the media and entertainment industries, intangible assets include publication rights and key talent employees. A musical artist's copyrights to their whole discography are an example of an intangible asset in the music business. Singers and musicians may also build a name for themselves in the marketplace.

The music production firm may have legal possession of the tunes, allowing them to collect royalties from their performance or sale. Even though these assets are intangible, they will ultimately be quite valuable to both the record label and the artist.

Consumer

In increasingly competitive industries, intangible assets like formula, recipe patents, and brand awareness are invaluable to consumer goods and service firms. The company's globally known brand name is an incalculable asset that drives the success and profits of Coca-Cola Company (KO).

Healthcare

Brand names, important workers, and the creation of new medications and treatment approaches are just a few examples of the industry's disproportionately high concentration of intangible assets.

Special Considerations

Additionally, the value of tangible assets is often fixed during their useful lifetime, making them the simplest to appraise. As they are depleted, tangible assets go from the balance sheet to the income statement.

A company's inventory, for instance, is a physical asset that, if depleted, must be factored into the cost of products sold. The term "cost of goods sold" refers to expenses incurred during manufacturing. The cost of goods sold accounts for stock depletion that occurs during manufacturing.
